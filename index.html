🧠 Cerebral Hemispheres & Topography

High-yield functional localization profiles for rapid clinical diagnosis mapping:

Frontal Lobe

  • Primary Motor Cortex (Precentral Gyrus): Somatotopic motor map (homunculus).
  • Broca’s Area (Inferior Frontal Gyrus - Pars Opercularis/Triangularis): Motor speech production. Lesion causes expressive non-fluent aphasia.
  • Prefrontal Cortex: Executive function, judgment, working memory.

Temporal Lobe

  • Wernicke’s Area (Superior Temporal Gyrus posterior): Language comprehension. Lesion causes receptive fluent aphasia ("word salad").
  • Hippocampus & Amygdala: Consolidation of long-term anterograde memories and emotional processing.

Parietal & Occipital Lobes

  • Primary Somatosensory Cortex (Postcentral Gyrus): Tactile, proprioceptive sensation processing.
  • Occipital Cortex (Calcarine Sulcus): Primary visual processing (V1). Geniculocalcarine tract terminates here.

⚡ Cerebrovascular Occlusion & Syndromes

Vascular territory mapping rules used during acute ischemic stroke assessments:

Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA) Occlusion

  • Contralateral hemiparesis and hemisensory loss affecting predominantly the face and upper extremity.
  • Gaze deviation pointing towards the side of the cortical lesion.
  • Dominant hemisphere involvement results in global/Broca/Wernicke aphasias. Non-dominant involvement causes hemispatial neglect.

Anterior Cerebral Artery (ACA) Occlusion

  • Contralateral motor and sensory deficits affecting predominantly the lower extremity (leg/foot homunculus map).
  • Abulia (delayed responses, lack of willpower) due to frontal lobe parameter damage.

Posterior Cerebral Artery (PCA) Occlusion

  • Contralateral homonymous hemianopia with macular sparing (due to collateral MCA circulation to the macular occipital pole).
  • Thalamic infarct presentation: Contralateral pure sensory loss.

🧬 Ascending & Descending Spinal Tracts

Crucial pathways for localizing traumatic spinal lesions and Brown-Séquard presentation:

Lateral Corticospinal Tract (Descending Motor)

  • Function: Voluntary fine-motor movement of ipsilateral limbs.
  • Decussation: Pyramidal decussation inside the lower medulla.
  • Lesion: Upper Motor Neuron (UMN) signs below lesion level (Spasticity, Hyperreflexia, Babinski (+)).

Dorsal Column-Medial Lemniscal Pathway (Ascending)

  • Function: Fine touch, vibration sense, conscious proprioception.
  • Decussation: Internal arcuate fibers within the lower medulla.
  • Anatomy: Fasciculus Gracilis (lower body T6 and below), Fasciculus Cuneatus (upper body limbs above T6).

Spinothalamic Tract (Ascending Sensory)

  • Function: Pain, thermal temperature perception.
  • Decussation: Anterior white commissure of spinal cord (crosses 1-2 segments above entry level).

👁️ Objective Cranial Nerve (CN) Testing

Standard bedside evaluation breakdown for emergency neurologic localizing:

Oculomotor Complex (CN III, IV, VI)

  • CN III: Controls SR, IR, MR, IO muscles, levator palpebrae, and pupillary constriction. Deficit shows "down and out" eye with severe ptosis.
  • CN IV (Trochlear): Controls Superior Oblique. Deficit causes vertical diplopia worsening on downward gaze (difficulty walking down stairs).
  • CN VI (Abducens): Controls Lateral Rectus. Deficit causes inward convergent strabismus and horizontal diplopia.

Trigeminal & Facial Complex (CN V & VII)

  • CN V Assessment: Corneal reflex afferent limb (V1), facial touch sensation mapping, jaw clenching strength.
  • CN VII Assessment: Corneal reflex efferent limb. Muscles of facial expression. Lower motor neuron lesion (Bell's Palsy) affects whole half of face; Upper motor neuron lesion spares the forehead due to bilateral cortical innervations.
